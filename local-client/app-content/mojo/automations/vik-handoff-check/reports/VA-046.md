Completed VA-046: Security decision engine

Claude's research output:
- Architecture: use a four-stage decision pipeline: deterministic policy evaluation, evidence synthesis, risk assessment, and approval routing/outcome.
- Policy layer: encode policies as declarative, auditable rules over actor, action, resource, and environment context.
- Evidence layer: gather structured signals such as authentication strength, user history, anomaly score, IP reputation, resource sensitivity, and time-of-access. Every evidence point needs provenance, freshness, source reliability, and confidence.
- Risk layer: combine evidence into transparent risk scoring that accounts for blast radius, severity, and time sensitivity. Coefficients and calculations must be logged.
- Outcome layer: map thresholds to allow, deny, conditional allow, or escalate, with approval workflows, SLAs, and fail-secure defaults.
- Guardrails: policy versioning and rollback, immutable decision logs, rate limits, approval timeouts, evidence staleness checks, conflict validation, coverage checks, and recurring audits.
- Eval/proof: replay historical requests, simulate policy changes before activation, measure decision accuracy, track approval override rates, monitor latency, and compare against manual or legacy rule-engine baselines.
- Product recommendation from Claude: build internally first for one scoped use case, then productize only if internal pilots prove multiple material use cases and owner sponsorship.

Vik's architecture-owner interpretation:
- This is one of the highest-leverage components in the whole backlog. A security decision engine can become the common allow/deny/escalate brain for AI-agent actions, human approvals, security operations, product runtime policy, and audit evidence.
- The engine must be explainable by construction. If it cannot cite the policy, evidence, risk calculation, and approval path that produced an outcome, it should not be trusted for autonomous or semi-autonomous security work.
- This should be deterministic at the boundary and probabilistic only inside evidence scoring. LLMs may summarize, inspect, or recommend, but the final decision should be made by explicit policy and recorded evidence.
- The architecture should support both product runtime decisions and internal control-plane gates, while keeping owner-specific policies separate from the shared engine.
