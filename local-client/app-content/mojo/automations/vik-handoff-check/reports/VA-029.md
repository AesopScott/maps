Completed VA-029: AI usage governance policies and approvals

Claude's research output:
- Architecture: AI usage governance needs policy layer, approval workflow, and exception handling.
- Policy layer: define risk tiers such as unrestricted, approved-use, and restricted. Policies specify model classes, permissible data types, approval requirements, audit obligations, and separate branches for internal vs customer-facing agents.
- Approval workflow: low-risk uses can auto-approve with logging; approved-use needs owner signoff; restricted use needs legal, security, and domain owner approval. Approval records should capture data classification, model, fallback, audit scope, decision, and rationale.
- Exception handling: temporary waivers and pilots need explicit scope, owner, sunset date, enhanced monitoring, and automatic review on expiry.
- Guardrails: API-level enforcement, model routing by approved use case, data classification at ingestion, blocking unapproved data, output filtering, tenant isolation, audit logging, owner clarity, escalation paths, revision cycles, shared policy registry, and developer-facing approval dashboards.
- Eval/proof: measure approval turnaround, exception success rate, compliance incident rate, policy-drift, approval gaming, monthly approval reports, quarterly behavior-vs-policy review, post-incident governance review, and 60-day dry run with internal teams.
- Product recommendation from Claude: build as an enabling component. Start internal, then customer-facing governance, then evaluate productization after proven use.

Vik's architecture-owner interpretation:
- Governance is not paperwork around AI products; it is part of the control plane. Policy, approval, exception, and evidence states must be machine-readable enough to influence runtime gates and human-readable enough for CISO/legal review.
- The strongest architecture is a policy registry plus approval ledger plus enforcement hooks. The registry says what use is allowed; the ledger proves who approved it; enforcement hooks stop unapproved model/data/action combinations.
- Customer-facing and internal agents need separate policy paths because exposure, liability, observability, and support obligations differ.
- Exception handling must be first-class. Most governance failures hide in permanent temporary waivers, vague pilots, or undocumented owner decisions.

- Why: every security-tool product we build will need AI usage approval, exception tracking, and compliance evidence. This becomes stronger when connected to trust-boundary design, data classification, RBAC, and decision engine work.
